Workers Through Time

  • 1895 The Bakeshop Act is passed, provisioning the maximum amount of time a bakeshop employee can work
  • 1905 The Supreme Court hears the case of Joseph Lochner versus the State of New York on the matter of the provisions put in place by the Bakeshop Act
  • 1916 The Keating-Owen Child Labor Act is passed prohibiting companies who use child labor from taking part in interstate commerce
  • 1935 FDR passes the Wagner Act as part of the New Deal in order to bring the United States out of the Great Depression
  • 1938 The Fair Labor Standards Act is passed, greatly increasing worker's rights and regulating business practices across the United States

FLSA

The Fair Labor Standards Act was signed into law by President Franklin Delano Roosevelt in 1938 as part of the new deal. The law provided several key protections for American workers. The regulations were able to be enforced by the federal government since they applied to employees of companies which carried out business across state lines, which is out of the jurisdiction of state governments.

The main provisions put in place for companies introduced by the FLSA were a federal minimum wage, mandatory "time-and-a-half" overtime for employees who work over 40 hours a week, and child labor regulations.

The fact that the act passed and was actually enforced by the government made life better for workers across the country as well as signifying a new age in the United States where the federal government would take a larger, more active, role in regulating the economy and the workplace.

The Wagner Act

As part of his New Deal President Roosevelt signs the National Labor Relations Act, or Wagner Act, into law. The law guaranteed workers the right to unionize. This included not only the right to form unions but the engage in collective negotiations and to strike.

The New Deal ushered in an era of hands-on regulations by the Federal Government on business in favor of employees.

The Bakeshop Act & Lochner v. New York

The Bakeshop Act was passed in 1895 and was an early attempt at regulating business practices across the country and instituting fair working conditions. The law set the maximum number of hours an employee in a bakeshop could work (10 hours a day/60 hours a week).

In 1905 the Supreme Court rules in favor of the plaintiff, Joseph Lochner, in the historic case of Lochner v. New York. The court ruled the enforcement of the Bakeshop Act unconstitutional under the 14th Amendment. This case set a precedent for courts using the 14th Amendments to kill regulatory legislation.

The Keating-Owen Act

The Keating-Owen Child Labor Act was signed into law in 1916 by President Woodrow Wilson. It's intent was to stop the gilded age practice of using child labor in order to lower payroll costs. The law was quashed in 1918 by the United States Supreme Court as unconstitutional under the 14th Amendment, continuing the trend started in the case of Lochner v. New York of using the 14th Amendment to kill legislation regulating businesses or enacting worker's rights.
